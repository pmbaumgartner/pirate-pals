# Clockwork & Campfires

**How to use this document.** Each principle is stated as a design rule, traced through the games that embody it, then split into *for making* (building it into your own games) and *for finding* (spotting it before you buy). At the end: productive tensions, lessons from near-misses, red flags, a storefront checklist, and a round-two shortlist.

---

## The Through-Line

Lay all 24 games on a table and three commitments emerge, and almost every game honors all three:

1. **Legible systems.** Games that show their work. Whether the resolution is dice (XCOM) or clockwork (Tactical Breach Wizards), the rules are knowable, the information is honest, and thinking is the core verb.
2. **Humane tone.** Comedy, warmth, or earnest tenderness — never cynicism, never grimdark posturing. Even the saddest games here (Spiritfarer, Frostpunk, Citizen Sleeper) are fundamentally about care.
3. **Respect for the player.** Respect for your intelligence (deduction, discovery, no hand-holding) and for your time (contained experiences, clean session boundaries, endings that exist and land).

The field tests exposed a fourth thread that was hiding in the original list all along: **your difficulty budget is spent on thinking, not on hands**. It's now Principle 10.

Just as telling is what's *absent*: no ranked competitive ladders, no battle royales, no open-world checklists, no horror-for-horror's-sake, no FOMO-driven live services, no 100-hour epics. Your library is a quiet manifesto against the attention economy's default game design.

## The Ten Principles

### 1. Decisions you can hold in your whole head

*XCOM: Enemy Unknown · Tactical Breach Wizards · Loop Hero · Defense Grid 2 · Frostpunk · Citizen Sleeper · Against the Storm*

The dominant genre in your library isn't shooter or puzzle — it's *deliberation*. Turn-based tactics, tower defense, indirect-control roguelites, dice-allocation RPGs, and now a settlement builder: all games where time pauses (or slows) while you weigh a small number of consequential options. Against the Storm fits the pattern precisely — you described it as an "exploratory RTS," and its genius is keeping the decision space small even in real time: a handful of buildings, three species with legible needs, one hostile forest. The decision space across all these games is tight enough to reason about completely — four soldiers, five towers, a handful of dice, one glade to open — but deep enough that the reasoning is genuinely interesting. Even your action games lean this way: Hi-Fi Rush quantizes chaos onto a beat grid, and Deep Rock Galactic's mining is spatial problem-solving as much as combat.

**For making:** Keep the number of simultaneous meaningful choices countable on one hand, then make each choice ramify. Depth should come from interaction between simple rules, not from volume of options. Surface every number the player needs; a decision made on hidden information isn't a decision, it's a guess with extra steps.

**For finding:** Look for the words *turn-based*, *tactics*, *deckbuilder*, *tower defense*, *roguelite builder* — but the sharper tell is screenshot legibility. If you can look at a still image and understand the state of the game, it probably respects your ability to think. If a screenshot is particle-effect soup, be suspicious.

### 2. Honest uncertainty: dice or clockwork, never a lie

*XCOM (dice) · Citizen Sleeper (dice) · Against the Storm (dice) · Tactical Breach Wizards (clockwork) · Defense Grid 2 (clockwork) · Return of the Obra Dinn (clockwork)*

You enjoy two opposite resolutions of uncertainty, and the pairing is instructive. XCOM makes drama from probability — the 85% shot that misses becomes a story you tell for years. Tactical Breach Wizards is its deliberate antithesis: Tom Francis has said his frustration with XCOM squad members dying to random chance and obfuscated information literally became the design document for TBW, which gives you full outcome previews and unlimited undo within a turn — you have complete agency over everything predictable, and the only unknown is what the enemy does *next* turn. Citizen Sleeper splits the difference: the dice are random, but you see your dice *before* allocating them, so randomness is input to your planning rather than a veto over it. Against the Storm works the same way — the forest's offers are random, but every trade-off is priced before you commit.

**The Islanders test.** Your Dorfromantik-versus-Islanders verdict confirms this principle operates even in the coziest genres. Islanders shows you the exact score a building will earn *before you place it* — the placement genre's version of a TBW outcome preview. Dorfromantik makes you infer scoring consequences from edge-matching and quest tiles, so feedback arrives soft and late. Same genre, same gentle tone; the one with the honest preview clicked and the one without it bounced.

What unifies everything you love here: **the game never lies**. XCOM's randomness is declared up front (drama comes from your consent to it); TBW's and Islanders' determinism is total and previewed. What you don't tolerate is the fuzzy middle — systems that pretend to be knowable but resolve arbitrarily or opaquely.

**For making:** Choose your uncertainty model deliberately and be loud about it. If dice: show the odds, honor them, and make failure survivable enough to be narratable. If clockwork: preview outcomes and give undo generously — Francis found that a forgiving sandbox turns mistakes into comedy instead of frustration. Wherever possible, show the consequence *before* the commit, even in cozy games. Never hide information to manufacture difficulty.

**For finding:** Reviews complaining about "unfair RNG" or "the game doesn't explain anything" are your specific poison. Reviews saying "every failure was my fault and I loved it" are your specific nectar. For placement/builder games, check a gameplay clip: does the UI show you what a move is worth before you make it?

### 3. The Click: insight must be earned, never given

*Outer Wilds · Return of the Obra Dinn · Animal Well · Portal 2 · Subsurface Circular & Quarantine Circular · The Council · Loop Hero · Against the Storm's glades*

The single most reliable pleasure across your list is the moment a mystery resolves *inside your own head* — before the game confirms it. Outer Wilds — now confirmed top-5 — is the medium's purest expression: there are no upgrades, no keys, no levels; the *only* thing that progresses is what you know, and the entire solar system is open from minute one. Obra Dinn engineered its most famous mechanic specifically to protect this pleasure: Lucas Pope's rule of three verifies fates only in batches, so you can't brute-force answers and confirmation always arrives *after* genuine deduction, never as a substitute for it. Animal Well runs the principle spatially, layering secrets so that finishing the game is merely the first stratum. Portal 2 runs it pedagogically: every chamber is a wordless lesson that lets you *feel* like the discovery was yours. Loop Hero and Against the Storm run it systemically — nobody tells you what happens when you ring a village with vampire mansions or crack open a forbidden glade; you find out.

**The Tunic Corollary: mystery in the world, clarity in the blockage.** Your Tunic experience draws the boundary of this principle with precision. You loved the concept and the first third, then hit areas where you couldn't tell whether you were on the wrong path, missing an ability, or simply not executing well enough — and the *ambiguity itself* is what discouraged you. The rule that falls out: **a stuck player should always know which resource they lack — knowledge, capability, or execution — even when the game refuses to say what the answer is.** Outer Wilds never has this problem because every wall is, by construction, a knowledge wall; being stuck is always an invitation to think, never a question of whether you're under-leveled. Animal Well stays almost entirely execution-light for the same reason. Tunic mixes knowledge gates, item gates, and combat-skill gates without labeling which is which, so curiosity curdles into self-doubt. Mystery about the *world* is delicious; mystery about the *nature of your own obstacle* is corrosive.

**For making:** Design the *confirmation delay* — the gap between "I think I've got it" and "the game says I've got it" is where the player's sense of intelligence lives (Obra Dinn's rule of three is a masterclass in tuning it). Give tools multiple undocumented uses. Teach by constructing situations, never tutorial text. And honor the Tunic Corollary: if you must gate by multiple resource types, make the *type* of each wall legible even while its solution stays hidden — a locked door says "capability," a riddle says "knowledge," and neither should secretly be the other.

**For finding:** "No hand-holding," "trust the player," "take notes on real paper," "knowledge is the progression system." Communities that beg newcomers to go in blind are a near-perfect signal. But check the corollary: search reviews for "didn't know where to go" and "hit a wall" — if stuck players describe *confusion about why* they're stuck rather than delicious puzzlement, expect the Tunic problem.

### 4. Warmth is load-bearing — and a multiplier on systems

*Portal 2 · Team Fortress 2 · Hi-Fi Rush · Tactical Breach Wizards · Lego Star Wars · Deep Rock Galactic · Spiritfarer · Firewatch · Harold Halibut · It Takes Two*

Your library is conspicuously *kind*. Half of it is laugh-out-loud funny — and notably, the comedy is always structural, not decorative. GLaDOS's insults are the reward loop of Portal 2. TF2's classes are comedic archetypes whose personalities *are* their gameplay identities. In TBW, the joke (throwing a man out a window) is also the tactical system, and Francis has said the team's rule was to make every interaction satisfying enough that emergent Looney Tunes moments earn a laugh while still making sense. The other half of the library is tender rather than funny — Spiritfarer, Firewatch, Harold Halibut, Citizen Sleeper — games about care, community, and loss that stay warm even when they're sad.

**The Into the Breach lesson.** Your field test produced the cleanest possible controlled experiment for this principle. Into the Breach is arguably TBW's mechanical superior — tighter, purer, perfect information distilled to chess density — and you *enjoyed* it. But TBW is the one that spoke to you, and you named the delta yourself: the dialogue, the story, the distinct characters and roles. Subset trims everything ruthlessly, including personality; their pilots are stat lines with portraits. The conclusion isn't that systems don't matter — it's that **warmth multiplies systems**. Near-identical mechanical virtue produced admiration in one case and attachment in the other, and the difference was voice. This is why "systems game with great writing" (TBW, Portal 2, Citizen Sleeper, Hi-Fi Rush) is the densest cluster of favorites in your whole library.

**For making:** Write the funny thing so that it's also the useful thing; a joke that lives in the mechanics is told every playthrough. Give every character a voice before you give them a stat block — a tactics game where units banter about the plan is worth more to this taste than one with a deeper skill tree. If the tone is sad, aim the sadness at care (what the player protects) rather than shock (what the game inflicts).

**For finding:** Watch three minutes of dialogue from any trailer or stream. If the writing has an actual voice — timing, specificity, jokes that land — buy with confidence. For systems games specifically, check whether the characters exist as *people* or as loadouts: the former predicts love, the latter predicts respect.

### 5. Together, against the game

*Deep Rock Galactic · It Takes Two · Split Fiction · We Were Here series · Portal 2 co-op · Lego Star Wars · GoldenEye · Star Wars Battlefront (Classic) · Team Fortress 2*

When your games are multiplayer, they are almost always *cooperative* or *couch-social* — shared victories, not extracted ones. Deep Rock Galactic's studio motto is literally "Co-op First," and Ghost Ship built the game to fix what they saw as Left 4 Dead's core flaw: veterans and newcomers couldn't stand on even footing. The result is a game where the community ritual ("Rock and Stone!") is a designed feature, celebrating teammates instead of ranking them. Hazelight designs from the same root — It Takes Two was built co-op-only from the first line of the design doc, with a Friend's Pass so the second player doesn't even need to buy the game — and Split Fiction (in progress with your wife, liked so far) carries the philosophy forward. The We Were Here series, now fully field-confirmed, adds a refinement worth naming: **conversation as the core verb**. Each player holds half the information, so the actual mechanic is talking — describing, confirming, misunderstanding, laughing. That's the couch-social energy of GoldenEye and Battlefront rebuilt as a puzzle system. Even your two competitive picks obey the rule: TF2's class interdependence makes teamwork the actual skill, and GoldenEye and Battlefront were competition as a way of being in a room with people you like.

One caveat from the field: Overcooked was liked, but its later levels demand an execution ceiling that starts to exclude — see Principle 10.

**For making:** Design roles that *need* each other (complementary verbs, asymmetric information, not four copies of the same soldier), make the failure state shared, and build celebration rituals directly into the input scheme — a salute button costs nothing and manufactures culture. Consider making communication itself the mechanic; it's cheap to build and generates the best moments. Never let one player's mistake give another player a reason to be cruel.

**For finding:** "Co-op first" or "built for two" in the developer's own words, PvE-only, a friend's pass or split-screen support, asymmetric-information designs. Avoid anything whose community discourse is dominated by rank anxiety.

### 6. Sessions with edges

*Deep Rock Galactic missions · XCOM missions · Defense Grid maps · Loop Hero loops · Citizen Sleeper cycles · Against the Storm settlements · Battlefront rounds — and, at the macro scale: Firewatch, Portal 2, Obra Dinn, Hi-Fi Rush, It Takes Two, Outer Wilds' 22-minute loop, the Circulars*

Everything you love is *shaped*. Your replayable games are built from crisp 20–60 minute units with a beginning, a climax, and a debrief — mission, map, loop, cycle, round, settlement. Against the Storm is practically this principle incarnate: it takes the most notoriously endless genre (the city builder) and cuts it into discrete expeditions that *end* — you win the settlement, collect the rewards, and the storm wipes the board. Even Outer Wilds obeys the rule at the micro scale: the 22-minute time loop gives an infinitely explorable mystery a hard session edge, so every loop is a complete sortie with a takeaway. Your narrative games are complete, authored experiences of 2 to 15 hours that end on purpose. The famous "one more turn" pull isn't addiction engineering; it's the honest product of clean boundaries plus a visible hook. Josef Fares is blunt about the macro version: it's far more common to feel a game is ten hours too long than too short, and he pushed It Takes Two to introduce new mechanics constantly because repeated mechanics become less special.

**The fizzle problem.** Dorfromantik's bounce adds a negative datum here too: its runs don't so much *end* as deflate — the tile stack slowly runs dry and the session dissipates without a climax or verdict. Islanders runs end decisively: you hit the threshold and sail to a new island, or you don't and the run is over. Sessions need edges on *both* ends. A defined start isn't enough; you want the drop pod, the storm, the stamped fate — a moment where the session closes and hands you its receipt.

**For making:** Define the session unit first and build the game around it. Every session should end with a *receipt* (loot, a stamped fate, a research unlock, a revelation) and dangle exactly one hook. Give sessions a decisive terminal event, not a slow fade. Cut the second use of any mechanic that isn't better than its first. Write the ending before you write the middle.

**For finding:** "How long to beat: 8–15 hours" is your sweet spot for narrative games. For replayables, look for mission/run/settlement structure with defined extraction — and check how runs *end*: a clear win/loss verdict beats a resource fizzle. Red flag phrases: "hundreds of hours of content," "endless," "daily rewards."

### 7. Good work is good play

*Hardspace: Shipbreaker · Deep Rock Galactic · Spiritfarer · Loop Hero · Against the Storm · Lego Star Wars · Animal Well*

A striking number of your favorites are, mechanically, *jobs* — salvage tech, miner, ferrymaster, viceroy of a rain-soaked logging camp. The pleasure is the pleasure of skilled labor: methodical process, tactile feedback, a technique ceiling, and a tidy completion state. Shipbreaker is the thesis statement: cutting a ship apart is meditative *because* the fuel lines and pressurized compartments make carelessness lethal — danger is what turns a chore into a craft. Spiritfarer makes cooking, weaving, and watering into acts of care with emotional weight attached. Lucas Pope, whose games are all jobs (border agent, insurance adjuster), put it best: he likes capturing the interesting parts of a mundane experience — people with boring jobs have interesting lives. Notice also that these games dignify the worker while satirizing the employer: Shipbreaker's crushing debt, DRG's cheerfully exploitative corporation, Against the Storm's demanding Scorched Queen, Obra Dinn's insurance bureaucracy. Blue-collar solidarity is a recurring flavor note.

**For making:** Give mundane verbs three things: technique (a right way and a better way), stakes (a way to get it wrong that matters), and closure (a visibly *finished* state — the stripped hull, the cleared cave, the completed settlement). Progress bars are the junk-food version; a clean workspace is the real thing.

**For finding:** "Zen," "satisfying," "meditative" plus some element of risk or mastery. Pure idle/clicker games will bounce off you — you want work, not watching.

### 8. Choices should leave scars — and scars should become stories

*Frostpunk · XCOM · Citizen Sleeper · Spiritfarer · The Council (acts 1–3)*

You are drawn to games where decisions are irreversible and the game *remembers*. 11 bit studios calls their philosophy "meaningful entertainment," and their design rules read like an annotation of your taste: no perfect solutions, every choice carries a cost, and the game refuses to judge — the goal is a question the player answers in their own head, capped by Frostpunk's ending asking simply whether it was worth it. XCOM gets the same effect through the memorial wall: a dead soldier with a nickname is worth more narrative than a hundred cutscenes. The Council's best trick — failed dialogue checks that *continue the story with consequences* instead of showing a game-over — is fail-forward design, and it's why acts 1–3 sang. Crucially, the stakes that move you are *human-scale*. Frostpunk 1 outranks Frostpunk 2 for you because FP1 asks "do I send this child into the mine," while FP2 asks "how do I manage this faction's approval" — the zoom-out from persons to populations traded empathy for abstraction.

**For making:** Prefer fail-forward over game-over; a failure that generates story is content, a failure that generates a reload screen is friction. Make consequences visible and permanent (a grave, a signed law that can't be unsigned, a spirit's empty cabin). Keep stakes attached to names, not numbers.

**For finding:** "Choices matter" is an empty phrase; look instead for "permadeath," "consequences," "no game over — the story continues," and reviews where people describe *guilt*. If reviewers are telling anecdotes about specific characters, the game generates stories.

### 9. The five-word premise, honestly executed

*Tactical Breach Wizards ("SWAT team, but wizards") · Deep Rock Galactic ("space dwarves mine together") · Hardspace: Shipbreaker ("dismantle spaceships in zero-g") · Spiritfarer ("ferry spirits to the afterlife") · Obra Dinn ("solve sixty deaths, one ship") · Outer Wilds ("solve a solar system's death") · Battlefront ("be a soldier in the movies") · Hi-Fi Rush ("the whole world hits on-beat")*

Nearly every game on your list can be pitched in one breath, and — this is the important part — the game then *keeps the promise completely*. TBW began as a joke name among PC Gamer writers and became a real design because the premise was load-bearing. Hi-Fi Rush's premise isn't a skin; the entire world, from platforms to dialogue to enemy attacks, actually pulses on the beat. Battlefront's promise was pure fantasy fulfillment — you've seen this battle in the theater, now you're *in* it — delivered with simple verbs and big scale. High concept, zero bait-and-switch.

**For making:** Write the five-word pitch first, then audit every system against it. Any mechanic that doesn't express the premise is scope creep wearing a lanyard. The premise should be visible in one screenshot and provable in the first ten minutes of play.

**For finding:** If the store-page description needs three paragraphs to explain what you do, pass. If the trailer makes you say the premise out loud to someone in the next room, buy.

### 10. Challenge the planner, not the pianist

*Evidence for: the entire deliberative library, DRG's even-footing design, TBW's penalty-free level skips and granular difficulty options, Hi-Fi Rush's forgiving beat windows and assists. Evidence against the alternative: Tunic's combat walls, Overcooked's late-game execution ceilings.*

Two of your field reports — from completely different genres — turned out to be the same complaint. Tunic discouraged you when progress started demanding combat execution you couldn't reliably distinguish from missing knowledge. Overcooked's later levels were liked less because they demand a dexterity ceiling (precise routing under time pressure, at speed, without error) that starts excluding one player in a pair. Put those next to what you love and the pattern is unmistakable: **your difficulty budget is spent on decisions, knowledge, and coordination — almost never on execution.** Nearly everything in your library is turn-based, pausable, undo-able, or forgiving. The exceptions prove the rule by how they handle it: Hi-Fi Rush is a rhythm-action game that never demands frame-perfection — off-beat inputs still work, on-beat ones are rewarded, and assist options abound. DRG has real aim-and-move demands, but Ghost Ship deliberately designed for even footing so a skill gap between friends doesn't ruin a session, and difficulty is chosen per-mission via hazard level. TBW lets you skip any level penalty-free. Where execution demands exist in your favorites, they are *floors you can always reach*, not *ceilings that gate progress*.

This is not "easy games." XCOM Impossible and Against the Storm's Prestige difficulties are brutal — but they're brutal to your *plan*. That's the distinction: you'll happily be outsmarted; you resent being out-fingered.

**For making:** Put the skill ceiling in decision quality, routing, knowledge, and coordination; keep execution floors low, forgiving, and learnable in flow (generous timing windows, undo, cheap retries, respawns). If a section must spike in execution demand, provide a route around it — a skip, an assist, a difficulty slider that modulates execution separately from decision complexity. In co-op, ensure a skill gap between partners degrades gracefully (the weaker player stays useful) rather than catastrophically (the weaker player becomes the reason for failure).

**For finding:** Interrogate *which kind of hard* a game is. "Brutal but fair" in reviews usually means execution; "every loss taught me something" usually means decisions. Check for assist/difficulty options and penalty-free skips. For co-op with your wife specifically: favor games where players contribute different *kinds* of effort (We Were Here's information asymmetry) over games where both must perform the same task faster and faster (Overcooked's endgame).

## Productive Tensions

Your taste isn't a single point; it's three spectrums you happily occupy both ends of. Knowing this stops you from over-filtering.

**Dice ↔ Clockwork.** You love XCOM's honest randomness *and* TBW's total determinism, Against the Storm's random offers *and* Islanders' previewed placements. The unifier is transparency; the enemy is obfuscation. So don't rule out a game for having (or lacking) RNG — rule it out for hiding the math.

**Cozy ↔ Crushing.** Spiritfarer and Frostpunk sit at opposite emotional poles, yet both are about the cost of caring for people. The unifier is *earned feeling*; the enemy is emotional manipulation and weightlessness. Your Disco Elysium report adds a practical footnote: weight without warmth is *mood-gated*. You appreciate Disco, but it waits unfinished because its density and bleakness demand you arrive with energy to spend. Frostpunk is equally heavy but sticky, because its weight is carried by systems and sessions rather than by prose you must be in the mood to read. Heavy games earn a place in your library; only *warm* or *structured* heavy games earn regular play.

**Guided ↔ Unexplained.** It Takes Two escorts you through 25 mechanics; Animal Well refuses to tell you what a single item does. The unifier is trust: the guided game trusts you to keep up with constant novelty, the opaque game trusts you to figure it out. What you reject is the patronizing middle — and, per the Tunic Corollary, the *disorienting* extreme: opacity about the world is a gift, opacity about the nature of your own blockage is a bug.

## What the Near-Misses Teach

Your caveats and bounces are as instructive as your favorites. Six case studies:

**The Council, acts 4–5:** the game abandoned the grounded social-deduction systems you'd invested in and sprinted through a supernatural lore-dump. Lesson: *the ending is part of the scope*. A rushed ending doesn't cost you one act; it retroactively taxes every act before it.

**Frostpunk 2 vs. Frostpunk 1:** competent, ambitious, and less affecting — because zooming out from individuals to factions swapped names for numbers. Lesson: *empathy doesn't scale*. If a sequel must grow, grow the systems, not the emotional distance.

**Tunic:** a beloved concept that curdled a third of the way in, when you could no longer tell whether you were blocked by missing knowledge, missing items, or missing execution skill. Lesson: *label the wall, hide the answer*. (Principle 3, Tunic Corollary.)

**Overcooked's back half:** shared joy that narrowed into an execution funnel. Lesson: *co-op difficulty should scale coordination complexity, not dexterity ceilings* — the moment one partner becomes the bottleneck, the design is extracting a cost from the relationship it was built to serve. (Principle 10.)

**Dorfromantik vs. Islanders: New Shores:** two cozy placement games; the one with previewed scoring and decisive run-endings clicked, the one with inferred scoring and fizzling sessions bounced. Lesson: *coziness doesn't exempt a game from honesty and edges*. Preview the consequence; end the session on purpose. (Principles 2 and 6.)

**Into the Breach vs. Tactical Breach Wizards:** the purer system earned a nod; the chattier one earned love. Lesson: *warmth is a multiplier, not a garnish*. When mechanics tie, characters break the tie — every time. (Principle 4.)

**Disco Elysium:** genuine appreciation, indefinite pause. Lesson: *weight needs a handle*. Emotional heaviness travels best inside warm characters (Spiritfarer) or crisp sessions (Frostpunk); heavy *and* dense *and* structureless means the game waits for a mood that rarely arrives.

## Red Flags (What Isn't in Your Library)

- Open worlds padded with copy-paste activities and map icons
- Competitive ranked ladders and any community organized around rank anxiety
- Live-service FOMO: battle passes with expiry dates, daily login rewards, seasonal pressure (note: DRG proves live content is fine when nothing expires and cosmetics are the only stakes)
- Hidden or dishonest math; difficulty via obfuscation
- Execution-skill spikes with no route around them — especially late-game spikes in otherwise-welcoming games
- Sessions that fizzle out instead of ending; runs with no verdict
- Ambiguous blockage: games where stuck players can't tell if they lack knowledge, gear, or skill
- Grimdark tone without warmth; edginess as a substitute for wit
- Tutorials that talk instead of teach
- Games that don't end, and games ashamed of ending

## Field Guide: the 60-Second Storefront Test

1. Can you state the premise in five words? (P9)
2. Does a single screenshot make the game state legible? (P1)
3. Session unit: missions/runs/settlements with clean edges *and decisive endings*, or amorphous grind? (P6)
4. HowLongToBeat under ~20 hours, or replayable in 30–60 minute units? (P6)
5. PvE / co-op / solo — not rank-driven PvP? (P5)
6. Which kind of hard is it — decisions or dexterity? Look for assist options, undo, penalty-free skips. (P10)
7. Do characters exist as people (banter, voice, arcs) or as loadouts? (P4)
8. Do reviews mention laughing, crying, or feeling clever — rather than "content"? (P3, P4, P8)
9. Do reviewers tell anecdotes about specific runs or characters? (P8)
10. When players report being stuck, do they sound puzzled (good) or lost about *why* (bad)? (P3, Tunic Corollary)
11. Any hint of "unfair," "cheap," "doesn't explain"? Walk away. (P2)
12. Strong committed art direction, not default-engine realism? (see every game on your list)
